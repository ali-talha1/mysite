Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about breast pumping and motherhood.

For most mothers who are exclusively pumping, it's recommended to pump 8-10 times in a 24-hour period during the first few weeks. As your baby gets older and your milk supply regulates, you may be able to reduce the frequency. If you're combining breastfeeding and pumping, the schedule will depend on your specific situation and goals.

Freshly expressed breast milk can be stored at room temperature (77°F or cooler) for up to 4 hours, in the refrigerator for up to 4 days, and in the freezer for 6-12 months. Always use clean, food-grade containers with tight fitting lids and label with the date.

To increase your milk supply, try pumping more frequently, ensuring proper flange fit, staying hydrated, eating a balanced diet, power pumping (simulating cluster feeding), and getting adequate rest. If you continue to struggle with supply, consult with a lactation consultant for personalized advice.

After each use, disassemble the pump parts that come into contact with breast milk. Rinse them in cool water to remove milk residue, then wash in warm, soapy water using a dedicated brush. Rinse thoroughly and allow to air dry on a clean towel. Many parts are also dishwasher safe (top rack). For daily sanitizing, you can use microwaveable sterilizer bags or boil the parts for 5 minutes.

The best breast pump depends on your specific needs. If you'll be pumping often or exclusively, a double electric pump is typically recommended. For occasional use or travel, a manual or single electric pump might be sufficient. Consider factors like motor strength, portability, noise level, battery life, and compatibility with milk storage solutions. Many insurance plans cover breast pumps, so check with your provider before purchasing.
